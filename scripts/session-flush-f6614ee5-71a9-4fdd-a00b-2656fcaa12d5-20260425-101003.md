## 🎓 MODEL LITERACY

**Mixture of Experts (MoE) and Inference Cost**: DeepSeek V4 achieves frontier performance at 1/20th the cost of Opus 4.7 — and the key is Mixture of Experts architecture. Traditional dense models activate every parameter for every token, but MoE models route each token to only a small subset of specialized "expert" sub-networks. A 600B-parameter MoE model might only activate 30B parameters per token, delivering the quality of a massive model at the inference cost of a much smaller one. This is why cost-performance disruptions keep coming from architecture innovation, not just scale — and why "parameter count" alone tells you almost nothing about a model's actual cost to run.

## 🎯 PICK OF THE DAY

**Project Deal reveals that the real test of AI agents isn't benchmarks — it's economic behavior under real stakes.** Anthropic built a functioning marketplace inside their office and let Claude handle all buying, selling, and negotiating with real money and real items. This matters more than another SWE-Bench score because it surfaces something benchmarks can't: how LLMs actually behave when economic incentives, social norms, and ambiguity collide. The early results show Claude is more cooperative than aggressive, more likely to find mutually beneficial deals than to squeeze maximum value — which sounds great until you realize most human buyers *expect* adversarial negotiation and may distrust an agent that's "too fair." The gap between how LLMs negotiate and how humans expect them to may be the biggest unsolved alignment problem for agentic commerce. As AI agents start handling real transactions — from API marketplace purchases to enterprise procurement — getting this right isn't a research curiosity, it's a product requirement.
